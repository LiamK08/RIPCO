The problem

The deadliest hazard at the beach is nearly invisible.

A rip current is a narrow channel of water flowing away from shore. It doesn't splash or roar — it often looks like the calmest gap between the waves. That's exactly what makes it dangerous.

Trained lifeguards spot rips by reading subtle patterns in the surf — a darker channel, a gap in the breaking waves, foam pulling out to sea. RipCo's computer vision is trained to read the same patterns, and to put that reading in your pocket before you reach the sand.

How it works

Trained eyes on the water, all day long.

  1. 01

    Plugged into public beach cams

    RipCo reads existing public camera feeds along the coast. No new hardware and no drones — the cameras are already watching the water.

  2. 02

    Computer vision reads the surf

    The model analyses the feed frame by frame, looking for the visual signature of a rip: a darker channel, a break in the whitewater, sediment streaming out to sea.

  3. 03

    A warning you can read at a glance

    When a likely rip is found, RipCo outlines the area in red on the live view with a confidence score — and plain guidance on what to do.

Read this before you swim

RipCo is an aid. It is never a verdict.

No app can tell you the water is fine to enter — and RipCo never will. Detection can miss rips, cameras can go down, and conditions change in minutes. Whatever the app shows, the rules below always apply.

  • Swim between the red and yellow flags

    That's the patrolled zone, chosen by lifeguards reading the water in person. If there are no flags and no patrol, don't swim.

  • Lifeguards outrank the app

    If a lifeguard's instruction differs from anything RipCo shows, follow the lifeguard. Every time, without exception.

  • Calmer-looking water is not clear water

    Rips often form in the smoothest gap between breaking waves — the spot that looks most inviting can be the one flowing out to sea.

  • If in doubt, don't go out

    No detection result, no green level and no calm surface changes this rule. When you're unsure, stay on the sand.

If you're ever caught in a rip

  1. Stay calm and float. A rip pulls you away from shore, not under the water.
  2. Don't swim against it. Even strong swimmers tire fighting a current head-on.
  3. Swim parallel to the beach until you're out of the channel, then angle back to shore.
  4. Can't get out? Keep floating, raise one arm and shout for help.

FAQ

Questions, answered straight.

Does RipCo replace lifeguards or beach flags?

No — and it never will. RipCo is an extra pair of eyes, not a verdict on the water. Lifeguards and the red and yellow flags are the standard you swim by; the app exists to back them up, warn you earlier and help you understand what they're protecting you from.

How accurate is the detection?

Every detection ships with a confidence score, and we publish how the model performs by beach and by conditions inside the app. But no model is perfect: RipCo can miss rips and can flag features that aren't rips. That's why nothing in the app is ever presented as an all-clear — treat every session as if a rip could be there.

Which beaches are covered?

Any beach with a public camera feed we can read. The in-app map shows live coverage, and you can request a beach — public cams are added continuously, prioritised by how many people ask.

Does it work without a signal?

The live camera view needs a data connection. The caught-in-a-rip guide and the flag rules are stored on your phone, so the guidance that matters most is always available — even with no signal on the sand.

Am I on camera? What about privacy?

RipCo analyses public, already-broadcast camera feeds, and the model reads the water surface — it doesn't identify people, track individuals or store footage of beachgoers.

How much does it cost?

RipCo is free to download, and core rip warnings for your saved beaches are included. We think a warning that keeps someone out of a rip shouldn't sit behind a paywall.
